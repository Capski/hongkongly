attractions

Discover the best of Hong Kong, from stunning views to vibrant street life

Landmarks

Victoria Peak & Peak Tram

Victoria Peak rises majestically to 552 meters above Hong Kong Island, offering the city's most iconic panorama across its glittering skyline and harbor waters. The historic Peak Tram, running since 1888, climbs at nerve-tingling gradients up to 27 degrees along its 1.4-kilometer route, transforming from a colonial-era coal-powered system to today's microprocessor-controlled funicular. As you ascend through dense subtropical forest, the city's architectural marvels gradually reveal themselves beneath you, culminating at the Peak Tower's wok-shaped structure where visitors can experience a truly breathtaking 360-degree vista that transitions from daytime grandeur to an evening symphony of lights.

Star Ferry Ride

The emerald-green Star Ferry vessels have been carving their path across Victoria Harbour since 1888, providing an essential maritime connection between Hong Kong Island and Kowloon. These distinctive double-ended ferries, with their wooden benches and open-air upper decks, offer an unparalleled 10-minute journey through one of the world's busiest harbors for just a few Hong Kong dollars. As engines thrum beneath you, the crossing delivers spectacular views of Hong Kong's dramatic skyline, from colonial-era clock towers to ultramodern skyscrapers that seem to defy gravity, all while cargo ships, traditional junks, and luxury yachts navigate the busy shipping lanes around you – creating an authentic Hong Kong experience that has remained essentially unchanged for generations.

Tsim Sha Tsui Promenade & Avenue of Stars

Stretching along Kowloon's southern edge, the Tsim Sha Tsui Promenade curves around Victoria Harbour offering uninterrupted views of Hong Kong Island's famous skyline. This waterfront pathway leads to the recently renovated Avenue of Stars, Hong Kong's tribute to its storied film industry and answer to Hollywood's Walk of Fame. Bronze handprints of cinema legends like Jackie Chan and Bruce Lee (whose life-sized statue stands in his famous fighting pose) line the promenade alongside QR codes linking to multimedia exhibits. The location provides the perfect vantage point for the Symphony of Lights show, when dozens of buildings across the harbor coordinate their illuminations to music each evening at 8 PM, creating a dazzling spectacle that transforms the cityscape into a canvas of synchronized light and color.

Ngong Ping 360 & Tian Tan Buddha

The Ngong Ping 360 cable car system delivers visitors on a breathtaking 5.7-kilometer aerial journey across Lantau Island's mountainous terrain, soaring above dense forest canopies and Tung Chung Bay's azure waters. Crystal cabins with glass floors heighten the dramatic 25-minute experience as the gondolas climb toward the cultural center of Ngong Ping Village. Dominating the landscape is the magnificent Tian Tan Buddha, a 34-meter-tall bronze statue completed in 1993 that sits atop 268 steps, representing man's journey toward enlightenment. The Buddha's right hand is raised in a gesture of blessing, while its serene face gazes north toward mainland China, surrounded by six smaller bronze deities offering flowers, incense, and other symbolic gifts to the enlightened one in a spiritual setting enhanced by mountain mists that often shroud the complex.

Street Markets in Mong Kok

Mong Kok's labyrinthine street markets transform the world's most densely populated district into a sensory explosion of commerce and culture, where neon signs cast multicolored glows across densely packed stalls. The Ladies' Market stretches over a kilometer along Tung Choi Street, offering everything from counterfeit designer goods to electronics alongside traditional silk garments. Nearby, Sneaker Street (Fa Yuen Street) displays thousands of athletic shoes in specialized boutiques where sneaker culture thrives amidst passionate collectors. As evening falls, Mong Kok transforms further at Temple Street Night Market, where fortune tellers set up alongside food stalls serving sizzling Cantonese delicacies like curry fish balls and stinky tofu, creating a perfect microcosm of Hong Kong's unique urban energy where ancient traditions and modern commerce converge in a captivating retail symphony.

Central to Mid-Levels Escalator

The Central-Mid-Levels Escalator system stands as an engineering marvel, stretching 800 meters through Hong Kong Island's steep urban landscape and rising 135 meters from Queen's Road Central to Conduit Road. Completed in 1993 at a cost of HK$240 million, this network of 20 escalators and three moving walkways forms the world's longest outdoor covered escalator system, transporting over 85,000 people daily. The system operates downhill from 6 AM to 10 AM to serve commuters heading to work, then reverses direction for the remainder of the day.
